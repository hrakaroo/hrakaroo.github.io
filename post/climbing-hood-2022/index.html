Climbing Mt. Hood 2022

Joshua Gerth
May 8, 2022 5 min read Hiking

Background

In March 2022 I was accepted into the Mazamas BCEP program where we learned everything from knot tying to rock and snow climbing. Once the program completed I signed up to join the Mt. Hood Climb scheduled for May 8, 2022 (mothers day). Due to weather conditions the climb ended up being pushed back to the following Wednesday (May 11th). Nine people (including the climb leaders) were in the climb group.

The day before

The day before the climb I drove up to Timberline lodge and parked in the lower parking lot that allows climbers to camp/park overnight. In the weeks before the climb I had built a flat “bed” in the back of my Honda Pilot so I could put a sleeping pad down and stretch out.

It’s a really good idea to get to the parking lot the night before and start to acclimate to the altitude. Timberline lodge is at around 6,000 ft of elevation and the top of Mt. Hood is at 11,249 so the more time you have to acclimate the better.

From the nine people in our group, six opted to show up the night before with three people driving up the morning of the climb. After rechecking my pack several times I went to sleep at around 8pm.

The day of

Getting Ready

Initially we were planning on starting our climb at 1am, but due to the temperatures being lower than expected we were able to push our start time back to 2pm.

As temperatures heat up it can cause the ice and snow to soften creating dangerous climbing conditions as rocks and ice have a better chance of breaking away and falling on you. Plus, it weakens the structure of snow bridges over crevasses which increases risk. Ideally you want to climb when everything is fozen solid and stable.

A start time of 2am meant we would ideally wake up at 1am to begin getting ready. Due to nerves I woke up closer to 12am and found myself unable to go back to sleep so at 12:30am I gave up trying and started to get ready. The day before I had prepared prepared oatmeal in a thermos with hot water which I had for breakfast and then started to get my gear on.

In addition to everything in my pack, I was also carrying one of the climbing ropes we were bringing in case we needed it going over the bergschrund. This added approximately 15 lbs to my pack.

Starting the climb

Just before 2am we started off. The night was amazingly clear but very cold. As we were hiking up we could see the head lights from other climbers further up the mountain who had left earlier than us.

The first part of the climb takes you to the top of the Palmer Lift and the route you take is along the “road” the snow cats create when coming back after grooming the ski slope. So the snow is pretty compacted and you can climb it easily in just your mountaineering boots without crampons. (Crampons are great, but they are heavy, make you prone to tripping, and generally slow you down.)

Silcox Hut

At approximately 3am we reached Silcox Hut and two things had already happened. First, two people decided to drop out of the climb as they were not feeling up to it. (It should noted that both of these people were from the group that decided to drive up the morning of the climb so so they did not have the extra six plus hours to acclimate to the altitude.)

Secondly, my water was starting to freeze. I had made the mistake of using a bladder for my water with an attached hose for drinking. This was the same setup I had used on multiple hikes and ski trips so I thought that as long as, after taking a drink, I blew air into my hose I could keep it mostly thawed. The problem was that the nozzle had collected water and was mostly frozen shut. After a lot of work I managed to get it somewhat unclogged and had, what would turn out to be, my last sip of water until after I was off the summit. In retrospect this was a huge mistake on my part and I should have carried backup water in my pack.

Somewhere between Silcox Hut and the top of the Palmer lift we started to get whiffs of the fumaroles on Mt. Hood which smell strong of sulfur.

Top of Palmer

At 4:40am we reached the top of the Palmer lift. The sun was starting to come up a bit but it was still really cold. For the climb up to the Hogsback (and summit beyond) we would be in deep snow and no longer on a groomed trail so we needed our crampons, helmets and grab our ice axe.

I was also able to switch off carring the climbing rope to another climber so for the rest of the time my pack was 15 lbs lighter which made a considerable difference.

We had brought snow shoes for the climb, but after looking at the steps already dug for us by the previous climbers, we decided to stash our snow shoes near Palmer and go on without them.

Hogsback

Once we reached the hogsback we were able to see the different routes to the summit. We decided that the Pearly Gates looked safe enough and the bergschrund was sufficiently covered that we felt comfortable climbing it without ropes. This allowed us to stash our ropes and harnesses which removed a considerable amount of weight.
